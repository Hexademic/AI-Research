Such feedback dynamics resemble echo-state networks or recurrent loops in neural circuits that keep a memory active.

• Exponential decay laws for forgetting (with extensions to multiple time scales or power-law decay, as some memory research suggests a double-exponential or power-law might fit forgetting curves better).
• Dynamical systems with feedback: e.g. Hopfield networks or recurrent neural networks that settle into attractors (content-addressable memories). Such models can produce non-linear retention – e.g., a memory trace might even grow stronger if periodically reactivated (spaced repetition effect), which one could model by making $\dot{M}$ positive whenever a recall happens.
• Oscillatory echo models: treating memory as a decaying oscillation or resonant circuit, capturing the transient “echo” of a stimulus in short-term memory.
By combining these, one can simulate a multi-scale memory: fast-decaying traces (seconds), longer-working memory loops (seconds to minutes, possibly via ongoing oscillations or persistent firing), and stable long-term memories (attractor states or slowly decaying weights over hours+). For instance, a bi-exponential model might use $M(t)=A e^{-\lambda_1 t} + B e^{-\lambda_2 t}$ to represent a quick initial drop (transient memory) and a slower component (consolidated memory). This is mathematically simple and can be implemented as two coupled variables (fast and slow memory) feeding into each other with differential equations. If emotional “resonance” is added – e.g. making $\lambda_1$ slow down when emotion is high – the memory of emotionally charged events will shift more into the long-lasting component, matching psychological observations. Overall, the mathematics of memory often boils down to dynamical systems (ODEs or discrete-time maps) with decay terms and reinforcement terms that can be tuned to produce realistic persistence and forgetting curves.
Temporal Waves and Narrative Self-Similarity
Cognitive processes often show rhythmic and repetitive patterns over time, which can be modeled using wave mathematics and self-similar structures. Wave-based time modeling treats the flow of experience or narrative as a superposition of oscillatory components. In practice, one can perform a Fourier transform on a time-series of cognitive state (or emotional state) to identify dominant frequencies – for example, circadian rhythms (~$1/24$h), ultradian rhythms, or faster neural oscillations (alpha, beta, gamma waves in the brain). These rhythmic components can be tied to cognitive functions (attention cycles, memory consolidation cycles, etc.). Maintaining rhythm coherence means that different processes synchronize their oscillations. Neuroscience provides evidence that synchronization across frequencies underlies integration of information: “communication through coherence” is a theory suggesting that neuronal groups communicate effectively when their oscillatory phases align. Thus, in an embodied AI, one might enforce a global clock or multiple harmonics that ensure different parts of the system resonate in unison at certain frequencies (e.g. a 10 Hz attention pulse coupling with a 5 Hz memory retrieval rhythm). Narrative continuity can also be captured by patterned recurrence in time. A story or an agent’s life narrative often has recurring motifs or states (akin to musical themes). We can use wavelet transforms or Fourier series to represent a narrative as a sum of periodic components, where coherence means those components reinforce a consistent theme. For example, if an AI’s cognitive state $X(t)$ is high-dimensional (various emotions, drives, etc.), one could project it onto a lower dimension (like a principal axis representing “mood”) and then analyze its frequency spectrum. Consistent identity might manifest as a dominant low-frequency component (a baseline “self” rhythm) with superposed higher-frequency fluctuations (momentary thoughts) that phase-lock with the baseline. The presence of a stable periodic or quasi-periodic component in $X(t)$ implies a repeating structure that could be interpreted as a narrative cycle or a sense of self that periodically reasserts itself.
Beyond periodicity, fractal structure provides a way to describe self-similarity across scales of time. Fractals are patterns that look similar at different magnifications. In a narrative or cognitive context, this could mean the agent’s micro-states (moment-to-moment thoughts) reflect in miniature the patterns of its macro-states (long-term goals or personality themes). Indeed, some storytellers and scholars describe stories themselves as fractal: the same themes and conflicts repeat in each chapter, act, and the story as a whole. To quantify this, one can compute the fractal dimension of a time series or trajectory. A classic result in complexity neuroscience is that human EEG/MEG signals exhibit $1/f$ spectral scaling (power-law decay in power spectral density), indicating self-similar (fractal) temporal structure in brain activity. Changes in fractal dimension or power-law exponent have been linked to different cognitive states and pathologies. For instance, higher fractal dimension (more complex, less predictable signal) often correlates with conscious, high-level cognitive processing, whereas a loss of complexity (more periodic or random) might indicate unconsciousness or disorder. If we treat “identity” as an emergent property of complex brain dynamics, we might say: a system that maintains a certain fractal complexity over time retains its identity, whereas a drastic simplification (loss of fractal richness) could correspond to a changed or diminished identity. In modeling terms, one could set a target fractal dimension or entropy for the system’s activity and include a feedback that steers it toward that operating point (e.g. near-critical dynamics, discussed below). This could be done by having the AI simulate dynamics near a self-organized critical state, where scale-invariant (fractal) patterns naturally occur. At criticality, the system shows power-law correlations in time and space – no single scale dominates, producing nested patterns (much like a fractal). This property might serve as a mathematical invariant for a stable yet flexible identity: the agent’s thoughts have variability on many timescales (preventing rigid periodicity) but also structured correlations (preventing complete randomness).
Topological invariants offer another structural lens. One can represent the evolving state of the system as a trajectory through a high-dimensional state space. The shape of this trajectory – its loops, twists, and holes – can be analyzed with topological data analysis. For example, if the agent’s state tends to cycle through a series of configurations and return close to its start (like a daily routine or a recurring mood cycle), the trajectory might form a loop. A loop is a topological feature that can be captured by the first homology group (an invariant indicating a hole in the state space path). Using techniques like persistent homology, we can detect such loops across different scales. A persistent loop in the state-space trajectory could correspond to a stable periodic behavior (a narrative loop or habit), which is an invariant of the agent’s identity. Topological invariants could also detect branching (if the trajectory splits, perhaps representing a change of narrative arc) or toroidal structures (if two or more independent cycles are running concurrently). While this is advanced, it provides a formal mathematical descriptor of narrative continuity: for instance, “the agent’s state trajectory has a genus-1 torus structure” might mean it has two independent cyclical processes (maybe two intertwined narrative threads) that continue indefinitely. These ideas are nascent, but tools from algebraic topology are indeed being applied to neural and cognitive data. In implementation, one might periodically take snapshots of the high-dimensional state (emotion, memory activations, etc.), embed those points in a point cloud and compute persistent homology to identify features like loops that last over time – those are your narrative invariants. The identity of the AI could then be defined as the collection of long-lived topological features in its state-space: e.g. one hole might represent a core conflict that it keeps revisiting, another might represent a stable preference cycle, etc. Maintaining identity might mean preserving those features (the system’s dynamics should not “fill in” those holes or create entirely new large-scale holes spontaneously).

Bridging Physical Computation and Lived Experience
Several mathematical frameworks explicitly attempt to connect low-level physical processes with high-level experiential or cognitive phenomena. One prominent example is Friston’s Free Energy Principle, which borrows from thermodynamics and information theory to explain brain function and by extension perception and emotion. In this framework, the brain (or AI agent) is modeled as trying to minimize a quantity called variational free energy, $F$, which is basically a measure of prediction error or surprise. This has led to equations where emotional valence (positive vs negative feeling) is linked to the rate of change of free energy: Joffily & Coricelli (2013) propose that valence = $-dF/dt$, so that if the agent’s predictions are getting better (free energy decreasing), it feels a positive reward, whereas rising free energy (increasing prediction error) yields negative affect. Here we see a direct mapping of a subjective feeling onto a mathematical term from physics. The Free-Energy Model of Emotion by Yanagisawa (2021) similarly formalizes arousal and valence in terms of information-theoretic free energy. The key equations come from Bayesian brain models: for example, the free energy can be written as or in simpler terms $F \approx (\text{prediction error})^2 + \text{uncertainty}$ under certain assumptions. The brain’s dynamics ($\dot{\theta}$ etc.) are then governed by gradient descent on $F$. This is physically inspired (minimization principle) and computationally realized (as a set of differential equations for belief updates). Such equations are well-suited to GPU implementation because they often reduce to matrix operations for prediction and error propagation. They also provide a bridge: terms like “entropy” and “energy” in the equations have analogs in lived experience (entropy might relate to boredom or randomness in stimuli, free energy to surprise, etc.).
Another physics-inspired bridge is the idea of criticality as mentioned above. The hypothesis is that the brain operates near a critical phase transition (between order and chaos), which maximizes information processing capacity. The mathematics of critical phenomena (Ising models, percolation theory, etc.) thus become relevant to consciousness. For instance, at criticality one sees scale invariance (power-law distributions of neural avalanche sizes/durations) and high dynamic range of responses. Some researchers have posited that losing consciousness (e.g. in anesthesia or deep sleep) corresponds to the brain moving away from criticality (becoming too ordered or too disordered). In equations, a system like a neural network can be tuned via a control parameter (e.g. synaptic gain or network connectivity) to a critical point. One can write an order parameter (like network synchrony or mutual information) and ensure the model hovers near the point where the derivative of the order parameter with respect to the control parameter is maximal (that’s the critical point). Self-organized criticality (SOC) models achieve this by local adaptation rules. For example, a simplified SOC rule: if activity is too high (supercritical), reduce coupling; if too low (subcritical), increase coupling, so the system self-tunes to the edge of chaos. The outcome is that the simulated AI exhibits 1/f neural noise, long-range correlations, and avalanche dynamics similar to brains. These phenomena are meaningful both physically (they indicate a critical system) and experientially (they correlate with rich, awake conscious states). So, implementing SOC in a GPU simulation (perhaps via spiking neural network models that add a synaptic scaling rule) lets the metaphor of “critical consciousness” play out in concrete computations.
Fractal and complexity measures also bridge the gap: as noted, one can compute the fractal dimension or Kolmogorov complexity of the agent’s activity as a single number. Some theories (e.g. entropic brain hypothesis) suggest higher entropy or complexity in brain activity corresponds to higher consciousness. So a designer might set a goal for the AI’s simulation to maintain a certain complexity level. The fractal dimension $D$ of an EEG time series, for instance, could be computed in real-time and fed back as a global variable the system tries to keep near an optimal value. If $D$ drops too low (system becoming too regular), a noise or chaos-inducing term could be added; if $D$ rises too high (system becoming too random), a stabilizing negative feedback could kick in. Thus a complex systems control approach uses mathematical diagnostics of the physical system (entropy, Lyapunov exponents, etc.) to modulate the system toward a desired qualitative state (like “alert consciousness” or “focused attention”).

Finally, frameworks like Integrated Information Theory (IIT) provide a bridge via information mathematics. IIT defines a quantity $\Phi$ that measures how much a system’s causal dynamics are irreducible to parts. The calculation of $\Phi$ involves constructing subsets of the system, computing entropies, mutual informations, and finding a minimum information partition. While IIT doesn’t give a single differential equation, it provides an algorithmic measure that connects the physical structure (network connectivity and logic) to a hypothesized level of consciousness. In practice, one could use IIT or related measures in simulation by computing $\Phi$ for the simulated voxel network and using it as a feedback signal. For example, you might adjust network parameters to maximize $\Phi$ (making the system more integrated) or to maintain a target range. This way, an abstract philosophical quantity becomes a computable objective guiding the physical simulation.
